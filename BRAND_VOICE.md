# Brand Voice — Norrœna Solutions

> The voice is editorial, considered, restrained, confident, and quietly precise. It is not breathless, not sales-y, not jargon-heavy, and not falsely modest.

---

## 1. The five voice rules

### Rule 1 — Precision over hype

**Yes:** "Sole HR specialist for a third-party administrator with ~350 employees across 31+ states."

**No:** "Scaled enterprise HR operations for a high-growth distributed workforce."

Specific numbers, specific scope, specific situations. Vague big-language ("scale," "enterprise-grade," "best-in-class") signals weakness. The buyer trusts precision because precision is harder to fake.

### Rule 2 — Italics are the only emphasis

The brand has one emphasis device: `<em>` rendered in bronze italic. No bold-for-emphasis. No ALL CAPS. No exclamation marks.

**Yes:** "Custom is the _core_ of what we do."

**No:** "Custom is the **CORE** of what we do!!!"

### Rule 3 — Em-dashes are encouraged

The em-dash — used to inject parenthetical detail or sharp pivots — is a deliberate editorial signal. Use them. They make the prose feel like it was written by a person who reads.

### Rule 4 — First-person where intimate, third-person where institutional

- **First-person** ("I built Bifröst because I needed it") is correct on the founder page, in the homepage manifesto pullquote, and in any direct-from-founder address.
- **Third-person** ("Norrœna delivers... ", "Each engagement names...") is correct in the practice page, case studies, and institutional copy.

Mixing the two within a single section is jarring. Be consistent within each section.

### Rule 5 — Honest disclosure is a feature

When something is sanitized, fictionalized, limited, or in-progress, **say so plainly**. The case studies page does this with the `※` confidentiality note. The founder page does it with the "practical note" on pronunciation. The audit form does it with the "no bot, no drip sequence" submit note.

Sophisticated buyers reward honesty. Acknowledging limits makes the firm sound more trustworthy, not less. The discipline cuts the other way too — if you don't have a real testimonial yet, the placeholder ("Testimonial pending publication consent — available on request under NDA") is itself a credibility move.

## 2. Forbidden words and phrases

These signal generic SaaS marketing. They are forbidden in body copy.

- "Solutions" (the word in the company name is fine; using it generically — "we provide solutions" — is not)
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Synergy"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Enterprise-grade" (use specific scope instead: "built for 350-person workforces")
- "Cutting-edge" / "state-of-the-art"
- "Streamline" (use the actual verb: automate, replace, eliminate)
- "Empower"
- "Game-changer"
- "Disrupt" / "disruptive"
- "Robust" (almost always vague)
- "Scalable" (claim a specific scale instead)
- "Innovative" (show, don't tell)

---

## 3. Encouraged words and phrases

These are on-voice. Reach for them when relevant.

- "Fixed-fee" (vs. "competitive pricing")
- "Sharply scoped" / "bounded" / "named"
- "Written" (acceptance criteria, scope, findings, report — never "documented")
- "Forged" / "built" / "delivered" (active making verbs)
- "Compounding" (a meaningful word in this business — every engagement makes the next faster)
- "Practitioner" (the founder is a practitioner, not a consultant or a vendor)
- "Plainly" / "honestly" / "directly"
- "Bounded" / "agreed" / "explicit"
- "Engagement" (vs. "project" or "contract")
- "Findings" (vs. "results" or "deliverables" — the audit produces findings)

| Pillar                   | Voice register                           | Why                                                                                                                       |

## 5. Headline craftsmanship

The display headlines on this site do a lot of work. Treat them as poetry, not as labels.

**Pattern 1 — The strikethrough**

```
"Your team is spending its talent on [work that software should be doing] —
and paying for tools that don't fit how they actually work."
```

The strikethrough is part of the rhetorical move. The reader's eye performs the deletion.

**Pattern 2 — The em-italic emphasis**

```
"Software built for the work you actually [do.]"
"Custom is the [core] of what we do."
"Begin with the [diagnostic.]"
```

The italicized word is always the _fulcrum_ of the sentence — the word that, if changed, would change everything.

**Pattern 3 — The em-dash pivot**

```
"Draupnir — the ring that drips."
"Michael Knudson — practitioner, engineer."
```

The em-dash introduces an apposition that defines or characterizes what comes before. Concise. Editorial.

**Pattern 4 — The fragment opening**

```
"A bridge between people and process."
"Four pillars. One discipline."
"Selected work, sanitized."
"A door, not a funnel."
```

Sentence fragments are encouraged in section labels and page subheadings. They feel considered, not chatty.

## 6. The wedge-offer language

The audit is the most important conversion target. Its language deserves special care:

- **Always called:** "HR Operations Audit" (full) or "the audit" (short). Never "Rún audit" in public-facing copy — that's internal vocabulary.
- **Never called:** "consultation," "assessment," "review," "discovery call." Those words signal something the buyer can get for free elsewhere.
- **The framing:** "Begin with the audit." Not "schedule a call." Not "request a quote." The audit _is_ the engagement; it's not preliminary to it.
- **The diagnostic question:** "Where is your team's time going, and what to do about it." Operations-first. Compliance is one dimension among several, not the headline.
- **The price:** $3,000–$4,500. State it. Don't hide it. Pricing transparency is a credibility move.
- **The credit:** "The audit fee credits forward into follow-on engagement." Always include this. It's the move that makes saying yes easy.

**Strategic note (2026-05-25):** This wedge offer was originally named "Multi-State HR Compliance & Process Audit" — the rename to "HR Operations Audit" was a deliberate repositioning to lead with the operations-first identity of the practice rather than compliance. Compliance literacy is the _substrate of credibility_, not the _product_. When in doubt about framing, lead with operations and let compliance show up as proof of depth.

## 7. Copy editing checklist

Before shipping any copy, check:

- [ ] Reads cleanly out loud
- [ ] No forbidden words (Section 2 above)
- [ ] Specific numbers where claims are made
- [ ] Italics for emphasis, never bold or caps
- [ ] Em-dashes used at least once (per major section)
- [ ] First-person or third-person consistent within section
- [ ] Active verbs (forged, built, delivered) preferred over passive
- [ ] No exclamation marks anywhere except in genuine speech
- [ ] Punctuation: smart quotes ("") not straight quotes (""), em-dash (—) not double-hyphen (--)
